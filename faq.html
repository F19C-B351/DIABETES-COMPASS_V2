🥗 Nutrition & Diet

What is the Glycemic Index (GI)? +

The Glycemic Index (GI) is a ranking system for carbohydrates based on how quickly they raise blood sugar levels after eating. Foods are categorized as:

  • Low GI (0-55): Slow digestion, gradual blood sugar rise (e.g., lentils, apples, Greek yogurt)
  • Medium GI (56-69): Moderate blood sugar impact (e.g., brown rice, whole wheat bread, oatmeal)
  • High GI (70+): Rapid blood sugar spike (e.g., white bread, white rice, baked potato)
Tip: Choose low GI foods most often to maintain stable blood sugar levels.
Which foods have a low glycemic index? +

Excellent low GI food choices include:

  • Proteins: Eggs (GI: 0), Greek yogurt (GI: 11), peanuts (GI: 14)
  • Vegetables: Lettuce, spinach (GI: 15), most non-starchy vegetables
  • Legumes: Chickpeas (GI: 28), lentils (GI: 32)
  • Fruits: Apples (GI: 36), oranges, berries

Visit our Nutrition page for a complete GI chart you can download.

What foods should I avoid or limit with diabetes? +

Foods to limit include high GI options that cause rapid blood sugar spikes:

  • White bread (GI: 75)
  • White rice (GI: 73)
  • Baked potatoes (GI: 85)
  • Cornflakes (GI: 81)
  • Sugary drinks and processed snacks
Note: You don't have to eliminate these foods entirely—just consume them in moderation and pair with protein or fiber to slow absorption.
How does insulin response relate to food choices? +

Insulin response measures how much insulin your body needs to process a food. High GI foods typically trigger a high insulin response, which can:

  • Cause energy crashes
  • Increase hunger shortly after eating
  • Make blood sugar management more difficult

Choosing foods with lower insulin responses helps maintain steady energy and better diabetes control.

🏃 Physical Activities & Exercise

What types of exercise are best for diabetes? +

We recommend a variety of diabetes-friendly activities:

  • Yoga: Gentle stretches that improve flexibility, reduce stress, and help manage blood sugar
  • Swimming: Low-impact, joint-friendly exercise excellent for cardiovascular health
  • Cycling: Great aerobic exercise that's easy on the joints
  • Running/Walking: Cardiovascular exercise perfect for diabetes management
  • Hiking: Outdoor activity that combines exercise with nature
  • Group Activities: Zumba, Tai Chi, water aerobics—fun and motivating

Explore our Physical Activities section for detailed guides on each activity.

How does swimming benefit people with diabetes? +

Swimming is excellent for diabetes management because:

  • It's low-impact and gentle on joints
  • Water supports your body, reducing injury risk
  • Great for those with neuropathy or foot issues
  • Improves cardiovascular health
  • Burns calories effectively
  • Cooling exercise—great for hot weather

Learn more on our Swimming page.

Why is yoga recommended for diabetes? +

Yoga offers multiple benefits for diabetes management:

  • Stress reduction: Lower cortisol helps regulate blood sugar
  • Improved flexibility: Gentle movements maintain joint health
  • Better sleep: Quality sleep improves insulin sensitivity
  • Mind-body connection: Increases awareness of your body's signals

Visit our Yoga page for beginner-friendly video tutorials.

What safety precautions should I take when exercising with diabetes? +
Important Safety Tips:
  • Check blood sugar before, during, and after exercise
  • Don't exercise if blood sugar is above 250 mg/dL with ketones or below 100 mg/dL
  • Always carry fast-acting glucose (tablets or juice)
  • Stay hydrated—dehydration affects blood sugar
  • Wear proper footwear and check feet after exercise
  • Wear a medical ID bracelet
  • Stop immediately if you feel dizzy, confused, or have chest pain
  • Talk to your doctor before starting new exercise programs
How often should I exercise with diabetes? +

General recommendations include:

  • Aerobic exercise: At least 150 minutes per week (30 minutes, 5 days)
  • Strength training: 2-3 sessions per week
  • Flexibility: Daily stretching or yoga
Tip: Start slowly and gradually increase intensity. Even a 10-minute walk after meals can significantly improve blood sugar control.

💊 Treatments & Medications

What are the latest diabetes treatment options? +

Modern diabetes treatment includes several approaches:

  • Oral Medications: Pills that help control blood sugar
  • Injectable Medications: GLP-1 agonists and other non-insulin injectables
  • Insulin Therapy: Various insulin types for different needs
  • Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGMs): Real-time blood sugar tracking
  • Insulin Pumps: Automated insulin delivery systems

Visit our Treatments page for comprehensive information on each option.

What is a continuous glucose monitor (CGM)? +

A CGM is a device that continuously tracks your glucose levels throughout the day and night. Benefits include:

  • No more finger pricks for routine monitoring
  • Real-time readings on your phone or receiver
  • Alerts for high and low blood sugar
  • Trend data to see how food and activity affect you
  • Share data with your healthcare team
Should I consult a doctor before making treatment changes? +

Absolutely yes! Always consult your healthcare provider before:

  • Starting or stopping any medication
  • Changing your insulin dosage
  • Beginning a new exercise program
  • Making significant dietary changes

Need a doctor? Use our "Need a doctor?" link to connect with healthcare professionals.
